# About This Site

This site is an awareness resource for the people who decide how Mountain Rescue Association (MRA) and Search and Rescue (SAR) teams allocate training time, certification pathways, and operational priorities.

It is intended to support informed discussion — not to replace existing training or certification systems.

## Overview

Washington's Search and Rescue (SAR) and Mountain Rescue Association (MRA) teams operate in some of North America's most demanding backcountry river environments, including the Cascade River in the North Cascades.

Most standard swiftwater training is designed for urban flood response — storm drains, infrastructure incidents, and vehicle rescues. These skills do not always translate directly to remote, high-gradient river environments.

This site examines that mismatch and its implications for training and operational readiness.

## In Scope

- Why urban swiftwater skills do not fully transfer to backcountry environments
- Plain-language overview of river hazards and the 1-10-1 cold-water principle
- Summary of the certification landscape for legal backcountry swiftwater operations in Washington
- A starting point for conversations around consistent volunteer training

## Out of Scope

- Step-by-step rescue instruction
- Certification delivery or courses
- Replacement for existing swiftwater rescue curriculum

## Who This Site Supports

**Mountain Rescue and SAR Teams (Washington State).** Decision-makers responsible for training budgets, operational readiness, and drill scheduling.

**Park Authorities and Land Managers (e.g., North Cascades National Park).** Organizations responsible for determining whether to invest in swiftwater rescue training for first responders operating in their jurisdictions.

**Other Interested Groups.** Swiftwater rescue advocates, canyoning communities, and recreational river experts with relevant watershed knowledge.
