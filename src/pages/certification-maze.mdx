# The Certification Maze

Running a legal swiftwater rescue in Washington requires a stack of
credentials distributed across multiple agencies, each with its own
curriculum, renewal cycle, and cost. For wilderness first responders
— MRA units and county SAR teams — the complexity of the stack is
itself a barrier to adding backcountry-specific training.

## Two entry paths, never one

To credential a team to run a swiftwater rescue in Washington, a
jurisdiction picks one of two entry paths:

### Urban
- **USAR (Urban Search & Rescue) / County Sheriff's Offices** —
  follows **WAC** (Washington Administrative Code) standards.
- **Fire Department** — follows **NFPA** (National Fire Protection
  Association) standards.

### Backcountry
- One of the above, **plus**
- **NASAR** (National Association for Search and Rescue) standards
  for backcountry specialty teams.

## Three certification levels per path

Each path runs across three levels of capability:

| Level         | Scope                                                   |
|---------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| **Awareness** | Recognize a swiftwater hazard; do not enter             |
| **Operations**| Shore-based rescue; throwbags, reach-and-throw, rigging |
| **Technician**| In-water rescue, boat-based, live-bait, complex rigging |

Every responder on a swiftwater team needs a clear level. Every team
leader needs to know what level every member is at. In practice, many
teams do not track this formally.

## The stack on top of the path

Beyond the entry path, a compliant responder also needs:

- **FEMA** laws — multiple, cross-jurisdictional.
- **WAC 118-04** — Washington-specific rescue standards.
- **NFPA 10-06** — fire service rescue standards.
- **CPR / First Aid** — baseline medical.
- **Rope tech** certification — rigging and high-angle work.
- **Swiftwater technician** specialty training — the river-specific
  piece.

A single responder legitimately certified to run in-water rescue on
a swiftwater operation carries half a dozen active credentials across
multiple agencies, each with its own renewal cycle.

## Why this is the wrong shape

Stacking unrelated disciplines to cover one job is the credentialing
equivalent of asking every rescuer to double- or triple-major in
undergrad. A cleaner design is a **single backcountry technical
rescue certification** that integrates the required components:

- Rope technician
- Swiftwater rescue
- Canyon rescue
- Glacier rescue
- High-elevation / steep-terrain rescue

One program, one renewal cycle, one clear standard for what a
backcountry rescue technician actually needs to do. That redesign is
out of scope for this project — but the complexity it describes is
part of why volunteer teams under-invest in backcountry swiftwater
training today.

## Who pays for certification

Funding patterns vary widely across the practitioner categories that
share the swiftwater curriculum:

| Who                                          | Who pays for training                                |
|----------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Recreational boaters                         | Out of pocket                                        |
| Commercial raft guides                       | Sponsored or funded by the outfitter (job-required)  |
| Volunteer SAR / MRA units                    | Often out of pocket; sometimes county subsidy        |
| Fire departments                             | Department budget — subject to being cut or grant-backfilled |
| Sheriff's offices / SAR teams                | County funding; in-house no-cost, external paid      |
| Utility and water agencies                   | Built into annual schedule, paid including time      |
| Military rescue teams                        | Federally funded                                     |

Two observations that matter for policy:

- **Funding fluctuates.** Departments have had swiftwater training
  removed from their annual budget and only restored via grants the
  following cycle. Certification stacks assume stable funding that
  does not always exist.
- **The highest-demand environment has the weakest funding path.**
  Backcountry water is disproportionately covered by volunteer units
  and recreational professionals, the two categories most often
  paying for their own training.

## The enforcement gap

Self-policing is the default. No external body routinely audits a
county SAR team's certification stack against its actual missions.
That creates a quiet third state alongside *certified* and
*uncertified*: **assumed certified**, where administrators have not
checked and responders have not flagged.

The absence of oversight is not neglect — there is no agency with
the mandate or the resources to audit. The effect is that the gap
between formal certification and operational reality stays invisible
until it appears in an incident report.

## What this means for the project

Wilderness first responders are not short on discipline. They are
short on a credential path that matches their actual environment.
Until that path exists, the most tractable lever is the one already
available to every MRA and SAR leader: prioritize consistent,
awareness-and-operations-level backcountry swiftwater training for
every member of the unit. See [The Bridge](/the-bridge) for how that
argument lands inside a training budget.
