# Get Involved

This site is an awareness resource. The people who can act on it are
the unit leaders, team leaders, and land managers who decide where
training time and budget go.

Three audiences, three different asks.

## If you lead an MRA or SAR unit

You are the primary audience. The resource is built around your
decision.

What helps most:

- **Put awareness-level backcountry swiftwater content on the
  annual training calendar** for every member of the unit. The
  [River Safety](/river-safety) page is a one-session starting
  point.
- **Name operational zones in your SOP** — Red (within 10 feet of
  moving water or on steep ground above it), Yellow (edge
  attendants), Green (staging / bystander) — so every mission brief
  can reference them by default.
- **Track certification levels explicitly** per member: Awareness,
  Operations, Technician. Pair each member's level with the zones
  they are cleared to work in.
- **Share incident retrospectives** where backcountry water
  capacity was the constraint. De-identified is fine.

## If you manage a park or jurisdiction with backcountry rivers

You decide whether responders under your agreement get
swiftwater-specific training and equipment.

What helps most:

- **Recognize the environment mismatch.** An urban swiftwater
  credential does not guarantee readiness on the specific river
  running through your park. See
  [What Is Backcountry Swiftwater Rescue?](/what-is-swiftwater-rescue)
  for the contrast.
- **Weigh training investment against activity restriction.**
  Restriction moves risk; training meets it.
- **Tell us what "trained" needs to look like** for you to route a
  call with confidence. Which credentials must be present? Which
  river-specific experience do you weight heaviest?

An example worth studying: park authorities at **North Cascades
National Park**, whose jurisdiction includes the **Cascade River**,
have a disproportionate exposure to high-consequence moving water
compared with neighboring parks — and a correspondingly higher
return on investment in swiftwater-trained first responders.

## If you work in certification, policy, or curriculum

You shape the standards the first two audiences operate inside.

What helps most:

- **Name the discipline in doctrine.** *River*, *whitewater*, and
  *swiftwater* belong in policy language alongside *alpine*, *rope*,
  and *avalanche*.
- **Distinguish urban and backcountry swiftwater** in training
  pathways, so units whose environment is the second aren't
  optimizing for the first.
- **Flag where the language fails you.** If "swiftwater rescue" is
  doctrinally absent from a unit's charter but operationally
  present in its mission logs, that mismatch is the gap this
  project exists to name.

## Contact

Reach this project through any existing WMRA contact channel and
mention backcountry swiftwater. Feedback, corrections, and
