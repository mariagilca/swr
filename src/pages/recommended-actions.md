# Recommended Actions

## What This Means for the Project

Wilderness first responders are not lacking discipline or capability. Instead, current certification systems do not always align cleanly with the operational environments in which they work.

Until more integrated credential pathways are developed, a practical step available to MRA and SAR leadership is to support consistent awareness- and operations-level backcountry swiftwater training across all team members.

## Stakeholders

### MRA and SAR Unit Leaders

These are the decision-makers responsible for annual training planning and operational readiness.

**Key actions.**

- Include awareness-level swiftwater training in annual schedules.
- Standardize SOP zones — Red, Yellow, Green — for mission briefs.
- Track member certification levels alongside zone clearance.
- Share de-identified incident reviews where water limitations affected operations.
- Allocate annual training time for awareness-level backcountry swiftwater content for all members.
- Establish operations-level training as a baseline for personnel working near moving water.
- Use existing in-water rescue curricula for technician-level development rather than creating parallel systems.
- Define operational boundaries (Red / Yellow / Green zones) within standard operating procedures for water-based missions.

### Park Authorities and Land Managers

These stakeholders shape local response planning and risk management strategies.

**Key considerations.**

- Recognize the environment gap: urban swiftwater credentials do not guarantee readiness for backcountry rivers (see [Why Swiftwater Rescue Is Different](/why-different)).
- Compare options: restricting access reduces exposure, while training improves response capability.
- Define "trained" for your jurisdiction: required credentials and river-specific experience needed for response readiness.
- Use awareness-level materials to establish a shared baseline vocabulary across staff and responders.

**Example.** North Cascades National Park has high exposure to technical moving-water environments, including the Cascade River, creating a strong case for investment in swiftwater-trained responders.

### Certification, Policy, and Curriculum Designers

These actors influence credential structures and training standards.

**Key actions.**

- Include river, whitewater, and swiftwater in doctrine alongside alpine, rope, and avalanche disciplines.
- Distinguish urban and backcountry swiftwater in training pathways to match operational environments.
- Identify gaps between doctrine and field operations where "swiftwater rescue" is absent from policy but present in mission activity.
- Explicitly recognize river, whitewater, and swiftwater rescue as distinct domains alongside other technical rescue specialties.
- Treat backcountry swiftwater rescue as a defined specialty area within broader rescue certification systems.

## Key Policy Observations

- Funding for training is often inconsistent over time. Budgets may be reduced or restored through grant cycles, which can create gaps in certification continuity.
- High-demand rescue environments frequently rely on limited or decentralized funding sources. Backcountry and wilderness swiftwater response is often supported by volunteer organizations or individual practitioners, which can increase the personal cost of maintaining certification.

## Why the Current Structure Creates Challenges

Maintaining separate certification tracks across multiple technical disciplines can increase training complexity and administrative burden for responders and agencies. This can also require individuals to maintain multiple overlapping qualifications across different renewal cycles.

Some integrated systems address this by combining core technical rescue disciplines into a unified backcountry framework, which may include:

- Rope rescue
- Swiftwater rescue
- Canyon operations
- Glacier travel and rescue
- Steep-terrain or high-angle rescue

A more integrated approach can reduce duplication, streamline renewal requirements, and provide clearer definitions of backcountry technical rescue capability. While developing such a system is beyond the scope of this project, the current structure helps explain why volunteer and rural teams may face barriers to expanding backcountry swiftwater training.

## What Success Looks Like

One year into deliberate investment in consistent backcountry swiftwater training, success would be indicated by several observable changes:

- Awareness-level backcountry swiftwater content is included in annual training cycles across participating units
- Operations-level training is consistently applied to personnel working near moving water
- The term "backcountry swiftwater rescue" appears in at least some MRA and county SAR documentation
- Post-incident reviews of water-related missions show more consistent use of shared terminology, hazard classification, and operational language across units

## Adjacent Work

Several complementary efforts support the broader goal of consistent backcountry swiftwater training:

- **Awareness-level learning materials.** Converting classroom-based water safety content into short-form media and knowledge checks, allowing in-person training time to focus more on field practice.
- **Field reference materials.** A concise, publicly accessible version of awareness-level content that can be reviewed prior to training or field operations.
- **Shared vocabulary development.** Establishing a consistent definition of "backcountry swiftwater rescue" to improve alignment across policy documents, training programs, and field operations.

## Why MRA Units Are the Right Venue

There are several reasons MRA units are a natural fit for strengthening backcountry swiftwater capability:

- **Jurisdictional alignment.** MRA teams are already primary responders for many backcountry incidents in Washington State.
- **Existing operational framework.** The principle that "people in the backcountry rescue people in the backcountry" extends logically to backcountry waterways, allowing training to build on existing capabilities rather than creating separate systems.
- **Established trust networks.** Land managers and agencies already coordinate backcountry rescue responses through MRA structures, providing an existing pathway for capability development.

## In One Paragraph

Washington's wilderness first responders are trained and committed, and many operate in environments that include significant moving water. However, the urban-centered swiftwater curriculum they most commonly encounter does not always reflect the conditions found in backcountry river environments. Consistent backcountry swiftwater training — structured as Awareness for all members, Operations for shore-based personnel, and Technician for in-water responders — represents a practical, scalable approach to improving readiness while working within existing organizational constraints. This approach supports both responder safety and public safety outcomes.
