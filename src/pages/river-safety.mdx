# River Safety

An awareness-level reference distilled from the **MRA River Safety
Training** material developed for Olympic Mountain Rescue, aligned
with the Mountain Rescue Association's backcountry water-safety
training standards.

> This page is awareness-level content. It does not substitute for a
> swiftwater rescue course. It helps a reader recognize moving-water
> hazards and decide whether to engage — and if so, at what level.

## Course objectives

- Promote proactive prevention of river accidents and injuries.
- Build an ability to identify and avoid river hazards by understanding
  hazards and river features.
- Focus on fast, low-risk strategies for the early management of river
  accidents.

## Working definition

> Moving water, per **NFPA**, is any water that exceeds **1.5 mph / 1
> knot**.

Any operation conducted near water above that threshold is, by doctrine,
a moving-water operation — regardless of what the unit charter calls it.

## River orientation

A few conventions that reduce ambiguity on scene:

- **River left / river right.** Always referenced *as if looking
  downstream*. North/east/south/west is unreliable because the river
  changes heading.
- **River miles.** Distance from the mouth upstream, marked on most
  navigable rivers.
- **Bends and eddies.** Named reference points on scene; useful for
  radio coordination and point-last-seen work.

## River features that make rapids

A rapid is the combination of four factors:

- **Obstacles** — rocks, wood, bedrock ledges.
- **Constriction** — the channel narrows and the water accelerates.
- **Gradient** — the slope of the riverbed.
- **Flow** — the volume (CFS) moving through the channel.

## Operational areas

Zones are a shared vocabulary for **where a team member is allowed to
be**, given their training and the PPE they're carrying.

### Red Zone — Over the Edge

- Within **10 feet of the water's edge**, or on steep slopes leading to
  the water.
- **Technician level** only. PPE required.
- Human factors: focus is on rescue. Pressure to enter is high.
- Unit role: disciplined execution within scope of practice.

### Yellow Zone — Edge Attendants

- Outside the Red Zone, but close enough to support rescuers at the
  water's edge.
- **Operations** and **Technician** level. PPE as needed.
- Human factors: desire to respond — the cognitive pull that gets people
  into the Red Zone before they're ready.
- Unit role: support, safety observation, rigging, communications.

### Green Zone — Staging / Bystander Area

- Out of both Red and Yellow zones.
- **Awareness** level, bystanders, family. PPE not required.
- Human factors: curiosity; hopelessness. Both can move people toward
  the water.
- Unit role: command post, stage equipment, manage people.

## Hazards with natural moving water

### The water itself

Hydraulic features — holes, pour-overs, recirculating eddies — are the
primary environmental hazard. Authoritative reference: *River Rescue,
4th Edition* — Les Bechdel & Slim Ray.

### Strainers

A strainer is any obstacle (typically wood) that lets water through but
catches a body. A strainer is the most reliably fatal feature on a
Pacific Northwest river. They do not look dangerous from the bank.

### Foot entrapment

Standing up in current above knee depth can trap a foot between rocks.
Once caught, the current folds the victim forward into the water. **Do
not stand up in moving current.** Defensive swimming position keeps feet
up and downstream.

## Water-related injuries

### Drowning

The terms to avoid: *near*, *dry*, *delayed*, and *secondary* drowning.
Current medical consensus (WHO / ILS) is to use a single term:
**drowning**, with qualifiers for outcome.

Industry shorthand to recognize (but avoid using in reporting):
**flush** — drowning with a PFD on, usually after long recirculation or
strainer entrapment.

### Cold water shock — the 1-10-1 Principle

The body's response to sudden cold-water immersion follows a predictable
timeline. Memorize it.

#### 1 minute to get your breathing under control

**Cold shock response.** An initial deep, sudden gasp followed by
hyperventilation up to 6–10× normal breathing rate. Keep your airway
clear and above water or drown. The response passes in about a minute.
Focus on avoiding panic and regaining control of breathing. A lifejacket
is critically important here — it keeps you afloat and breathing while
the response passes.

#### 10 minutes of meaningful movement

**Cold incapacitation.** Over 5–15 minutes, effective use of fingers,
arms, and legs is lost. Concentrate on self-rescue first. If that isn't
possible, prepare a way to keep the airway clear while waiting for
rescue. *Swim failure* occurs in this window. Without a lifejacket or
something to hold, drowning is likely.

#### 1 hour before unconsciousness due to hypothermia

**Hypothermia.** Even in ice water, unconsciousness typically takes
approximately 1 hour. Knowing the aspects of hypothermia — techniques
to delay it, self-rescue, calling for help — dramatically increases
survival and rescue outcomes.

### Musculoskeletal injuries

Common patterns, grouped by swimming posture:

- **Defensive swimming** (feet up, on back, downstream) — tailbone and
  hip impacts.
- **Offensive swimming** (head-first, aggressive angle) — head, torso,
  and hip impacts.
- **General** — ankle/leg, arm/wrist, shoulder dislocations.

Plus:

- **Entrapment** injuries.
- **Hypothermia.**
- **Exposure.**

## Mission planning near moving water

### Callout information

- Subject location, access points, known point last seen.
- Water-related context: is the subject reported in the water, near it,
  or unknown?

### Team lead responsibilities

- Identify all waterways in the mission area.
- Determine expected water conditions — river stage, temperature, recent
  precipitation.
- Set **operational team boundaries** before launch (Red / Yellow / Green).
- Complete a **SAR GAR** (Green-Amber-Red risk check).

### SAR near rivers — search methodology

Point-last-seen work shifts when the subject may be in or near moving
water:

- **What was the person's objective?** Fishing, hiking, photography —
  each implies different river proximity.
- **What gear did they have?** PFD, drysuit, none.
- **Rescue or recovery?** Informs search urgency, not search scope.
- **Heads up or heads down?** Changes body drift.
- **Where will the subject end up?** Down river or on the bottom.
  Consider:
  - Outside of bends (strainers).
  - Large eddies.
  - Sandy beaches for tracks.
- **How far can they go?** Set downstream containment accordingly.
