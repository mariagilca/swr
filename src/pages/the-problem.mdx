# The Problem

Washington's wilderness first responders — Mountain Rescue Association
(MRA) units and county Search and Rescue (SAR) teams — are highly
capable. They answer calls in terrain the urban fire service does not
go. And on backcountry water, they are asked to apply a skillset built
mostly for a different environment.

That environment mismatch is the problem this site names.

## 1. Urban and backcountry swiftwater are not the same job

The national swiftwater rescue curriculum was built around **urban**
scenarios: urban flooding, storm drains, submerged vehicles, rescues
from structures. Representative techniques include things like
**elevator rescues** from flooded buildings — work that assumes
infrastructure, road access, and a relatively short walk from the
truck to the patient.

Backcountry swiftwater is a different animal:

| Factor               | Urban swiftwater                    | Backcountry swiftwater                     |
|----------------------|-------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Typical scene        | Flood, storm drain, submerged car  | Remote river, creek, or canyon             |
| Access               | Road; often paved                   | Trail, off-trail, or water-only            |
| Time to patient      | Minutes                             | Hours                                      |
| Water character      | Turbid flood, relatively slow       | Cold, clear, high-gradient, technical      |
| Primary hazards      | Debris, entrapment in structures    | Strainers, foot entrapment, hydraulics     |
| Communications       | Reliable radio / cell               | Patchy to nonexistent                      |
| Watercraft required  | Inflatable, jetboat                 | Rafts, kayaks, packrafts, catarafts        |
| Support infrastructure | Full dispatch, mutual aid          | Small unit, remote, self-sufficient        |

An elevator rescue skillset does not transfer to a Class IV creek at
mile marker twelve. Neither does a storm-drain skillset. The physical
movement patterns, the read of the water, and the search methodology
are all different.

## 2. Volunteer teams carry the mission in backcountry water

Most backcountry water response in Washington is handled by volunteer
MRA units and volunteer SAR teams, not career fire departments.
Volunteer status is not a weakness — it is why these teams exist in
the places where career departments do not. It does mean:

- Training budgets come from grants, counties, and out-of-pocket
  spending.
- Drill time competes with every other specialty the unit carries
  (rope, alpine, wilderness medicine, avalanche).
- Consistency of training across members depends on who can afford
  what, when.

## 3. Urban funding does not fix backcountry problems

National swiftwater rescue funding flows heavily through urban fire
service budgets. Urban departments are generally less concerned with
moving-water training because their geography does not warrant it.
The exceptions — towns like **Leavenworth**, where the Wenatchee
River runs through the center — prove the rule.

The outcome is predictable. Backcountry rivers are where swiftwater
fatalities concentrate, and backcountry training has the thinnest
funding path.

## 4. "We're already capable" is the silent constraint

High-performing rescue teams earn the reputation they carry. That
reputation is also the thing most likely to delay a conversation
about new training. The internal logic is understandable:

> We run technical rope. We run alpine. We run medical. We get the
> job done with what we have. Swiftwater is one more specialty — and
> we've done okay without it.

"Okay" is load-bearing in that sentence. Many Washington units have
run water-adjacent missions without incident — until the year they
did not. That is the quiet shape of the problem: it is invisible on
the mission log until the day it is not.

## 5. "Don't know what they don't know"

Because certification is self-policed and oversight is thin,
administrators are often unaware of gaps in their own teams. The
analogy offered during our research: an undergrad walking across the
stage still short of graduation requirements — no one flagged it, and
the system kept moving. Teams that have not run a serious backcountry
swiftwater mission rarely find out what their gaps are until they do.

## 6. The doctrine does not match the terrain

The MRA's own policy documents historically do not use the words
*whitewater*, *river*, or *swiftwater* — but MRA units' mission logs
do. That mismatch between doctrine and reality is where this project
lives.

## What the numbers say

From an internal audit conducted for Olympic Mountain Rescue and
public recreation databases:

- **196** Olympic Mountain Rescue mission reports contain the word
  *river*.
- **378** listed paddling sections of whitewater in the American
  Whitewater database for Washington.
- **305** listed swiftwater canyons in the RopeWiki database, **35
  of them in the Olympics**.

The canyon and river counts grow year over year as more of the state
gets documented and paddled. The chance that a Washington mission
involves moving water is high, and the chance that it is urban is
low.

## A concrete example: North Cascades

Consider **North Cascades National Park**. The park is home to the
**Cascade River**, one of the top whitewater rivers in North
America. Compared to Mount Rainier, where glacier-fed rivers are
rarely paddled, North Cascades draws a higher volume of
whitewater-capable recreationalists — and with them, a higher rate of
moving-water incidents.

Park authorities at North Cascades weigh two options when incidents
rise:

1. Restrict high-risk river activity.
2. Invest in swiftwater rescue training for the first responders
   attached to the park.

The second option is the one this site argues for. Restriction moves
risk elsewhere. Training meets the environment as it is.
