# The Project

Washington is home to some of the most demanding backcountry water in
North America. The North Cascades alone drain dozens of whitewater
rivers, including the **Cascade River** — one of the top whitewater
runs in the country. When a recreationalist goes under on one of these
rivers, the call goes to the nearest Mountain Rescue Association (MRA)
unit or county Search and Rescue (SAR) team.

Those teams are highly trained. They are also trained mostly for
**urban** swiftwater scenarios — the flooding, storm drains, and
stranded-vehicle work that dominates the national curriculum. That
curriculum does not transfer cleanly to a remote Class IV creek.

This site exists to name that gap, show why it matters, and make the
case for consistent backcountry swiftwater rescue training for the
wilderness first responders who already own the mission.

## What this site is

An awareness resource. It is aimed at the people who decide how MRA
and SAR teams spend their training time and budget.

It is **not**:

- A step-by-step rescue manual.
- A certification course.
- A replacement for any existing swiftwater rescue curriculum.

It **is**:

- An honest map of why urban swiftwater skills do not transfer to
  backcountry environments.
- A plain-language overview of river hazards and the 1-10-1
  cold-water principle.
- A breakdown of the certification landscape that governs who can
  legally run a swiftwater rescue in Washington.
- A starting point for a conversation about consistent volunteer
  training in backcountry swiftwater rescue.

## Who it's for

**Primary audience.** Mountain Rescue Associations and Search and
Rescue organizations in Washington State. The people deciding where
training dollars and drill days go.

**Secondary audience.** Park authorities and land managers — for
example at **North Cascades National Park**, whose jurisdiction
includes the Cascade River — who weigh whether to invest in swiftwater
rescue training for the first responders who answer calls on their
waterways.

**Tertiary audience.** Swiftwater rescue advocates, canyoning
communities, and recreational river experts whose watershed knowledge
intersects with this work.

## The short version

Wilderness first responders in Washington carry the mission for
backcountry water incidents. The urban-oriented swiftwater curriculum
they typically train on does not match the terrain they actually work
in. Consistent backcountry swiftwater training — built on the same
awareness, principles, and hazard literacy this site introduces — is
a low-cost, high-leverage investment in responder and public safety.
